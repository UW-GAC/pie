How to apply tags

  • From a study variable's detail page
    1. Find a study variable by searching within your study or browsing within your study’s variables or datasets.
    2. Once you’ve found a study variable that you’d like to apply a tag to, click on the variable’s name and you will be taken to its detail page.
    3. Click the "Tag this variable" button. You will be taken to a page to apply a tag to this study variable.
    4. In the "Tag" field, you may either select a tag from the dropdown menu or start typing to autocomplete the tag by name. When autocompleting, hit return to select the tag of your choice.
    5. Click on the "Save" button to apply the selected tag to the specified study variable.
  • From a tag’s detail page
    1. Find one or more variables by searching within your study or browsing within your study’s datasets. You may also search your study's variables on dbGaP.
    2. Copy and save the dbGaP accession (phv) or variable name of the variable(s). You will use this information at a later step.
    3. In the navbar, select “Phenotype tags” > “Phenotype tags”.
    4. From the list of tags, click on the name of the tag you’d like to apply to a study variable. This will take you to the tag’s detail page.
    5. Click the 'Tag variables as "[tag name]"' button. You will be taken to a form to select study variable(s) to tag.
    6. In the "Variable(s)" field, paste or start typing the study variable's dbGaP accession (phv) or name that you saved earlier. Hit return to select the study variable of your choice.
      • Example: You want to select “MF353” from Framingham Heart Study, phv00000803. You can type or paste several things to select the study variable.
        • phv803
        • 803
        • 00000803 (note that the number of leading zeros is important)
        • MF353
    7. You may select multiple study variables for the "Variable(s)" field. All of the selected study variables will be labeled with the selected tag.
    8. Click on the "Save" button to apply the selected tag to the selected study variable(s).
  • From the “Apply tag to variables” form
    1. Find one or more variables by searching within your study or browsing within your study’s datasets. You may also search your study's variables on dbGaP.
    2. Copy and save the dbGaP accession (phv) or variable name of the variable(s). You will use this information at a later step.
    3. In the navbar, select "Phenotype tags" > “Apply tag to variables”. Or click here to go directly to the form.
    4. In the "Tag" field, you may either select a tag from the dropdown menu or start typing to autocomplete the tag by name. When autocompleting, hit return to select the tag of your choice.
    5. In the "Variable(s)" field, paste or start typing the study variable's dbGaP accession (phv) or name that you saved earlier. Hit return to select the study variable of your choice.
      • Example: You want to select “MF353” from Framingham Heart Study, phv00000803. You can type or paste several things to select the study variable.
        • phv803
        • 803
        • 00000803 (note that the number of leading zeros is important)
        • MF353
    6. You may select multiple study variables for the "Variable(s)" field. All of the selected study variables will be labeled with the selected tag.
    7. Click on the "Save" button to apply the selected tag to the selected study variable(s).
{# Guidelines #}

General guidelines for tagging

  • Refer to a tag's instructions for specific details and examples of which study variables are appropriate under this tag.
    • Apply the tag to all of the study variables that fit the description and instructions
    • Apply the tag to study variables from all time points and all repeated measures within each time point.
    • Apply the tag to both corrected & uncorrected, adjusted & unadjusted study variables.
    • Apply the tag to all instances of duplicated study variables.
      • e.g. "Visit" or "Subcohort" included in multiple data sets.
  • Unless otherwise noted in tag instructions, a study variable should usually only have one tag applied to it.
  • Some study variables (composites) are calculated or inferred from a combination of other variables (i.e. components).
    • Unless otherwise noted in tag instructions, do not apply a phenotype tag to component study variables that could be used to derive the phenotype.
    • e.g. height and weight are component variables used to derive the body mass index (BMI). Do not apply the "BMI" tag to height or weight variables or vice versa.
  • Do not tag medication variables with their related phenotype tag; instead, there is a general "medication use" concept with which all medication use variables should be tagged.
    • e.g. A study variable for lipid-lowering medication use should be tagged as "medication use" and not as "LDL in blood" or "HDL in blood", etc.
  • Disease/event/intervention/behavior phenotype tags:
    • Status concepts include prevalent and incident diseases (e.g.asthma, COPD), events (e.g. MI), medical interventions (e.g. angioplasty, CABG), and behavior (e.g. smoking). Status variables are qualitative indicators of whether individuals have the specified disease, event, intervention, or behavior. Status may be based on self report, adjudication, clinical verification, medical records, ICD code, or by calculation/inference from related traits. Tag both prevalent and incident case variables. Include both event status and time-to-event variables for incident cases. Include variables that give age at diagnosis/event/intervention/behavior (e.g. answer to question "how old were you when you first were diagnosed with asthma").
  • Multiple tags for the same study variable:
    • In general, the phenotype concepts are defined with enough specificity to avoid multiple tagging of a single study variable. However, there will be exceptions. A clear exception is when a single study variable represents multiple distinct phenotypes. For example, consider a data set containing blood chemistry results for a number of subjects. This data set has multiple rows per subject with columns named subjectID, analyte (blood analyte type such as LDL, fibrinogen, IL6), and concentration (measurement of concentration of the corresponding analyte). For rows in which the analyte column value is "LDL", the concentration column is a measure of LDL concentration; for rows in which the analyte column value is "fibrinogen", the concentration column is a measure of fibrinogen concentation; etc. In this example, both the analyte and concentration variables should be tagged with all three phenotype concepts ("LDL in blood", "Fibrinogen in blood", and "Interleukin 6 in blood").
{# Age #}

Special instructions for the Age at enrollment/collection tag

We consider two types of age variables:

  1. Participant age at enrollment or age at which data or biosamples were collected.
    • Examples: age at enrollment, blood draw, sample collection, imaging, visit or exam identifier, and days since baseline visit.
    • Include all study variables representing age directly and all study variables used to calculate age (such as days between exams or visits).
    • These types of variables usually provide age for multiple different phenotypes. For example, age at blood draw corresponds to age at measurement of all blood chemistry analytes assayed from that blood draw; age at clinic visit for imaging provides age for all traits derived from the images, etc. These types of age variables should be tagged with the "Age at enrollment/collection" tag.
  2. Participant ages that correspond to the timing of specific events, diagnoses or behavior.
    • Examples: age at onset of asthma symptoms, age at smoking initiation, and time in time-to-event data.
    • These types of variables are tagged with the corresponding event/diagnosis/behavioral concept. For example, age at onset of asthma symptoms should be tagged with "Asthma status", age at onset of smoking should be tagged with "Cigarette smoking", time to event where the event is COPD should be tagged with "COPD".
{# Terminology #}

Terminology

  • Tag
    A tag is a label representing a phenotype concept. This is usually synonymous with a phenotype name. Click on a tag's name to see its detail page, where you can read the detailed description of the phenotype concept it represents and specific instructions for identifying study variables to apply the tag to.
  • Tag description
    A brief description of the phenotype concept that the tag represents.
  • Tag instructions
    Detailed instructions for what types of study variables to apply the tag to. Includes common examples of study variables to include or exclude from tagging.
